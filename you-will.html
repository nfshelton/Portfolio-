you will.

After doing a case study of the Adidas Runbase Berlin website, my teammate and I created a brand book, sample website, and sample app to address the pain points we researched. Thus rebranding the site into our own site that promoted the same fitness and health classes, naming our company - You Will.

Research

Pain Point 1: The website needs to have a page that displays all the classes in a more straight forward manner.

Besides the inconvenience of the layout, there is a color associated with each class but the colors are never explained throughout the website, and thus have no significance. When we interviewed the staff, they said the website was confusing and that they really wanted to change it to make it more understandable.

Pain Point 2: The website needs to appeal to a broad range of people from all suitable ages and fitness backgrounds.

When we interviewed staff at the Runbase, and asked them if the classes were for all intensity levels, they assured us that they were. However, when we considered taking a class, we were extremely hesitant and intimidated because we did not match the fitness levels of everyone their site was showing.

Website Sample

Our initial application flow had a separate route for first-time users because we thought setting up the profiles was a critical step of using the app. The user profiles will consist of the database of the whole matching, so it was crucial to make setting process easy and simple to get the users onboard.

As we moved forward to the onboarding process, we realized that we should prioritize our focus on the matching itself, feeding with pre-set profile. We pivoted our application flow to only prototype for returning users, and not first-time users.

We have learned that keeping focus on the main feature of an app helps anchor the app development.It assures that everything else revolve around the center, to assist and make sense of the main feature of matching between people and projects.

User Testing

We did two sets of user testing. The first was with our initial low-fi prototype, and we took the feedback and made the suggested changes. After that, we made a high-fi prototype, and got user testing on that. The most important feedback we got was:

• Swiping can be confusing and conflicting with browsing and expanding profiles
• Features that help filter the feeds was commonly requested
• While sending out requests, an optional tag-along message would be helpful
• Confusion on the matching process and criteria on having chats and matches

High-Fi Prototype

Hi-Fi Prototype Summary

There were three main flows that we wanted to emphasize the interactions between. The first being the landing page. Assuming you already had your account, you could easily see what projects interested you, as well as the Profiles of people that could work on your project. You have the ability to quickly request collaboration or ignore people, or if you wanted more information you could see their full profile.

Entering into the full profile flow, the user can see the main skills that the applicant wanted to emphasize, as well as their portfolio pieces. In this case Marquise had three pieces he wanted to share, and he was able to write a summary of what they were and include relevant media. You could also choose to request and ignore the applicant here, and upon requesting you can write them a note so they have more information about you. The last flow is the messaging flow. This is where you can chat with people in-app, and see collaboration requests, as well as the people or projects that you have requested and reached out to.
